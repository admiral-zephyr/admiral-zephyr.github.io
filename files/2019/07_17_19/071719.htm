Ultimate Robot Fighting League
   Robot Wars first aired on British television in 1998, soon afterwards, in 2000, it was picked up in America as BattleBots. For many this was their first taste of robot fighting. The whole history of robot fighting is as fuzzy as the history of robots itself. In 1987, the Denver Mad Scientists Club began their first Critter Crunch at MileHiCon. It was simple by today's standards. It was held in a hotel, on a table, and one of the rules was not to damage the surface of the 'arena'. The combatants consisted of an amalgamation of parts from a steel mixing bowl, a RC Dune Buggy and a RC Corvette. The Corvette won. As simple as it was, it still drew a crowd of around 200 people.
   Mainstream popularity of these televised competitions has waxed and waned to the current day but many other robot battles have been held off the air. Unfortunately, many annual events are short-lived, usually lasting only a few years... One of the longest running competitions is the Robot Battles, an untelevised and direct descendent of the original Critter Crunch. It follows pretty standard rules and it's robots look pretty similar to the ones on the TV shows.
   Robots fit into one of four weight classes, up to one pound, one to three pounds, three to twelve pounds and twelve to thirty pounds. A robot may only compete in one weight class. No cluster bots or robots that seperate. No internal combustion engines. No liquids. And most of all, nothing hazardous to the judges, spectators or opposing operators. Don't be an ass to win at any cost basically.
   If you are interested in building a robot for competition, Robot Battles is where you'd want to start. They offer guides on the basics and are probably the most organized group of this nature. It might not be much but it's at least a starting point.
   If modifying roombas is a little too basic for you, there's other types of robot fighting that may suit your sophisticated palate. Robo-One features bipedal bots with less focus on destruction. It's closer to boxing or kendo where the point is to score a hit on your opponent or knock it down. It has a much larger set of rules than Robot Battles. Robots must have two arms, two legs, a head and a torso, and that's just the beginning of the regulations. Center of gravity, angle of joints and length of limbs are major factors in these competitions.
   There's also autonomous robot sumo. Robots are placed in a ring and once the round starts there can be no outside help. The robot is on it's own to push out its opponent. This might sound a lot like Robot Battles but these little devils are crazy fast. Darting edge to edge of the ring can happen a few times a second and matches are usually over in a matter of seconds.
   Maybe you want giant robots, like something out of Mechwarrior? Megabots challenged Suidobashi Heavy Industries to a duel. Megabot's Eagle Prime is a 6-ton 15-ft tall robot with a pilot onboard. Suidobashi's Kuratas is about 5 tons with a height of around 12-ft. The fight was more like a mix between WWE and a monster truck rally. It was more of a publicity stunt than an actual competition.
   Hopefully in the future we have more robot competitions to look forward to. There's a lack of information out there and only a handful of groups actually organizing events. Eventually, I imagine we'll see destruction derbies with self driving cars and quadcopter dogfights. Who knows where this robot craze will take us.
