What a Long, Strange Trip It's Been
   World of Warcraft launched on November 23rd, 2004, and at this point, there really isn't anyone who hasn't heard of it. WoW was a little bit of a dark horse when it was first announced. At the time, Everquest was the leading MMORPG and there was a handful of others, like Ultima Online and Dark Age of Camelot. Blizzard had experience with multiplayer games because of Battle.net hosting games for Diablo, Starcraft and Warcraft, but having thousands of players actively participating in one world would be a feat.
   When news of Blizzard's new MMORPG first started spreading, I was only mildly interested myself. I loved the Diablo games and dabbled in some of the others but the idea of World of Warcraft didn't really draw me in. Slowly, as more and more information came out, I found myself looking forward to playing and would research which race and class I wanted to start off as. Signing up for the closed beta did me little good but I did manage to play the final open beta. Instead of the Night Elf Druid I was planning on, I tried a Night Elf Hunter. It was amazingly polished for a beta and I was mesmerized as I explored every nook and cranny of Teldrassil, the Night Elf starting area.
   This was the one World of Warcraft launch I missed. I didn't get a copy until the middle of December. It was the last box on the shelf and I ended up sharing the account with my two other friends so we could all play. We all started on the Alleria server as Alliance and I picked a Night Elf Hunter again because of the fond memories of the game that I had in the beta. Playing all through Christmas break, I eventually had to go back to college. Even though I reached level 40-something, all of my other friends at college had started on the Hellscream server and played Horde. So to play with them I had to start over on this new server... This was pretty common when WoW first launched. Any time you would meet someone in real life that played, you would state your faction and server.
   Starting fresh on a new server would be a chore but I did it anyways and rolled a Troll Rogue. This time I slowed down my levelling and with this stealthy rogue I was able to explore unimpeded by monsters. I would end up level 30-40 for about a year since I was never much of a hardcore player and there was so much to do in Azeroth. Hopping from guild to guild, I eventually hit the max level at the time of 60. In August of 2006, I founded the Carpe Noctem guild, which is still active to this day. Though I founded it, I don't consider myself much of a leader and passed the role to a more responsible person soon afterwards. The title of Guild Master has been handed down to the next ever since.
   We had the same nostalgic experiences you hear about from other veteran WoW players, raiding Tarren Mill for fun, toxic Barrens chat, Alterac Valley battleground lasting days, and the Gates of Ahn'Qiraj locking up the server. Staying up late and being too drunk to raid are some of my fondest memories of this game. Unfortunately, none of the original members remain. That's just what time does. Moves on.
Burning Crusade (January 16th, 2007)
   The first WoW expansion introduced us to two new races, the Draenei and the Blood Elves. The level cap was increased to level 70, added flying mounts and it also let Paladins play on Horde and Shamans play on Alliance. A new profession, Jewelcrafting, was added. There was also the new Eye of the Storm battleground, world pvp events, and 2v2, 3v3 and 5v5 arenas. A new zone, Outland, gave WoW a little bit of a science fiction vibe and many people started referring to the Draenei as 'Space Goats'. The expansion was well-received and brought many more players to the game.
Wrath of the Lich King (November 13th, 2008)
   This second expansion gave us a new class, the Death Knights. They were everywhere... You couldn't find a dungeon group, raid or battleground without at least one person playing Death Knight. The level cap was increased to level 80 and another new zone was added, this time as the continent of Northrend, home of the Lich King. Another profession was added, Inscription. A new type of pvp was added, Wintergrasp, where the whole area was designed specifically for pvp. Every two and a half hours a new battle between the Alliance and Horde would break out. The winners from the last fight would control the fortress where the entrance to a short pve raid would take place. The losers from the previous match would be on offense, smashing walls with siege weapons and trying to overtake the fortress's current owners. This was another great expansion for many people and added to WoW's fame.
Cataclysm (December 7th, 2010)
   By the third expansion, WoW was starting to show it's age. It not only added two more races, the Goblins and the Worgen, but also reforged the world in Deathwing's fire. Outland, Northrend and everything those expansions introduced remained the same but all other zones were redesigned. There was no new continent this time but new zones were added to the Eastern Kingdoms and Kalimdor. Flying was added to every zone except the Draenei and Blood Elf starting zones. Archaeology was added as a secondary profession and the level cap was increased to 85. Even though Cross Realm grouping was added, compared to the previous expansions this one was a little mediocre for players.
Mists of Pandaria (September 25th, 2012)
   In the fourth expansion, they added the Pandaren race and the Monk class. The new Pandarens started neutral but could later choose between the Alliance or Horde. Challenge Mode, Scenarios and Flex Raids were introduced but what really blew everyone away was Pet Battling. Another continent was added, Pandaria, as well as several smaller islands, and the level cap was increased to 90. This expansion was a step up from the last one and brought many fresh players to the game.
Warlords of Draenor (November 13th, 2014)
   The fifth expansion is often considered the least entertaining... Increasing the level cap to 100 and adding player housing called Garrisons did little to keep players around for the whole expansion. New mechanics, like followers and random item upgrades, started here. Draenor is the new continent this time and it's based on what the Outland would look like in an alternate timeline basically. No new classes or races were added in this expansion though they did update some models of the original races. Overall, this expansion was mediocre as well.
Legion (August 30th, 2016)
   Everyone was ready for the sixth expansion to start. The level cap increased to 110 this time and the Demon Hunter class was added. Garrisons became Order Halls based upon class and players were given upgradeable Artifact Weapons based upon their class specialization. Mythic+ dungeons were introduced as a way to scale dungeons past normal, heroic and mythic difficulties. The Broken Isles, and later Argus, are the new areas this time. This expansion brought everyone back to the game and was quite entertaining in general.
Battle for Azeroth (August 14th, 2018)
   Now we're on the seventh expansion. The current level cap is 120 and Allied Races have been added, a kind of subrace of the classic races. Adding Void Elves, Lightforged Draenei, Dark Iron Dwarves and Kul Tiran Humans for the Alliance, and Nightborne Elves, Highmountain Tauren, Mag'har Orcs and Zandalari Trolls for the Horde. Instead of having a shared continent, this expansion gives each faction their own. The Alliance receive Kul Tiras and the Horde gets Zandalar. Factions aren't restricted to their own continent but encouraged to invade and set up bases in enemy territory. The new Warfronts are a kind of pve battleground and Island Expeditions are a kind of replacement to Scenerios. The Nazjatar and Mechagon zones have been added as well. Since this expansion is ongoing and I'm coming back from haitus, it's a little difficult to sum up this expansion but it's been pretty good so far.
World of Warcraft Classic (August 27th, 2019)
   It's been almost fifteen years since the original World of Warcraft launched and now we're getting a remastered version. Rumored to start at patch 1.12, WoW Classic will be doled out in phases starting with Molten Core, Onyxia and Maraudon. There will be no honor system yet but pvp just for fun will be possible. There aren't many details out yet but Blizzard has allowed reserving names to prepare for launch. The game should function just like it did back in the day, bugs and all, with few modern comforts patched in. Luckily, it won't be a seperate subscription fee and anyone with an active WoW subscription will have access.
   There's a huge nostalgia factor at play here but how well will modern gamers acclimate to the days of yore? What lacking feature will torture us the most? There's a lot of people prepared to have a go at it again but there will be a very niche audience that sticks around for more than a month. I've already thrown my hat into the ring and reserved my name but I'm not certain how long I'll stick with it.
