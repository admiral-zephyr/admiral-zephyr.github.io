Google-Fu




   Everyone googles things nowadays. So widely used that it's even become a verb. And while not everyone uses the Google search engine, they do google things on other sites like DuckDuckGo or Yahoo. Everything is at our fingertips these days. Mere seconds from an answer to nearly any question. Search engines, and computers in general, have gotten more intuitive and quicker but people are still pretty bad at using them...
   The first thing I'd suggest is learning the shortcuts, usually they do the same thing across multiple programs as well as multiple operating systems. This might seem a little basic for many but I'm going to explain this for all audiences. Even if you know these shortcuts, use them more and develop some muscle memory so it becomes second nature.

   The <TAB> key is probably one of the most useful keys to use. With <ALT>+<TAB> you can switch applications quickly. With <CTRL>+<TAB> you can switch between tabs in the active application. And with <TAB> and <SHIFT>+<TAB> you can select the next field or previous field in forms, or select links in a webpage with <ENTER>.
   The <SHIFT> key and the mouse will let you select virtual objects in a range. If you have 10 pictures in your file manager, click the first one and then <SHIFT>+<LEFT-CLICK> the last picture. This will select items 1 to 10. Or you can select 1 to 9 or 3 to 7. Everything from the first selected to the last selected.
   The <CTRL> key and the mouse acts similar but only selects the items you click. With those same 10 pictures, if you <CTRL>+<LEFT-CLICK> pictures 2, 5 and 7 then only 2, 5 and 7 will be selected. Get used to using <SHIFT> and <CTRL> with your mouse, they are used often in many different programs but are rarely made apparent.
   Next on my agenda is using better search terms when googling. This is a little more universal than computer shortcuts. Every word is a precise idea, just like in the real world. A boat can be a rowboat or a schooner. A dog can be one of hundreds of breeds. There's a big difference between duck, dodge and avoid. They can all mean the same thing but at the same time can mean something entirely different from each other.
   Using a <+> or <-> can give a word more priority or remove the term from searches. For example, if what you're looking for must contain the word dodge but you don't want the car manufacturer you could search for +dodge -car and narrow your search results.
   With the pipe <|>, or vertical bar, you can select multiple words interchangeably. You can also use OR with similar results but only if capitalized. When or isn't capitalized, you can get different results. Using duck|dodge|avoid, duck OR dodge OR avoid and duck or dodge or avoid all yield different results.
   You can also use quotation marks <"> to find exact phrases or words with spaces. Putting all of these together can narrow down searches from the millions to a couple dozen results if used correctly. Searching for -dodgeball +"dodge ball"|+dodge-ball will remove every reference of dodgeball as a single word and only look for compound words with or without the hypen.

   And now for some of the more advanced search operators. The bulk of searches are just to find the URL of a website that people are just too lazy to type in the URL bar themselves, like facebook, amazon or netflix. But there's a ton of smaller sites that host all sorts of information and with these you can dig really deep and find some of the most obscure things imaginable.

   site: will show only results from a specific website or type of website. With this you can block out every other website containing similar subjects. Maybe you only want to search government sites (.gov) or just github websites (.github.io). Or you could do the opposite with a <-> and remove websites you've seen a thousand times from showing up in your searches.
site:<Website>
site:cnn.com florida
-site:youtube.com cat|kitten
site:.gov laws

   filetype: will let you search for files of a certain type, like PDFs or text files. This doesn't include image files but there's a list of indexable file types you can search for. You could use this to find manuals, books, source code or templates for projects. And again, with the <->, you could stop PDFs or other files from popping up.
filetype:<File Extension>
filetype:pdf bonsai
filetype:kml oklahoma
filetype:docx application

   inurl: is similar to the site: search but will find matching searches from the whole URL and not just the domain of the website. The difference in these two operators will let you find a /cnn/ directory on a website that isn't cnn.com for example.
inurl:<URL>
inurl:browse
inurl:/administrator/index.php
inurl:/cnn/ -site:cnn.com

   intitle: will search just for the title of a website, usually between the <title> and </title> of the HTML's source code. This is handy because a lot of websites have default titles for certain pages, especially with the Internet of Things taking over more and more devices.
intitle:<Title>
intitle:"traffic cameras"
intitle:liveapplet
intitle:index.of./

   Hopefully this will help you google a little better and if it doesn't help very much then you should google for better information. Grow your lexicon of search terms and definitions, learn synonyms and antonyms of as many words as you can. This isn't like a genie where you only get three wishes, you can keep trying and maybe get more refined results. Eventually you'll find exactly what you're looking for.
