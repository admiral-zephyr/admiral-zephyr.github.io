Break Stuff
   Not in the Limp Bizkit white trash anthem kind of way though. There's been a growing trend of companies to lock consumers out of their own devices. At what point is your stuff even your stuff anymore? Our electronics are coming equipped with more and more sophisticated software and hardware locks designed to keep users from tampering.
   This fight has been going on for decades now. The Electronic Frontier Foundation, or EFF, was founded in 1990 and in 1995 sued the US government on behalf of Dan Bernstein. Bernstein was developing his 'Snuffle' encryption system and planned to publish a paper and the related source code. However, the US government had placed encryptions and software with encryption capabilities on the United States Munitions List. Publishing a paper on encryption was akin to exporting weapons. While the regulations were loosened, Dan Bernstein tried to challenge these regulations again in 2003. The case was dismissed and Bernstein was invited to try again when the government made a "concrete threat".
   On October 28th, 1998 the Digital Millennium Copyright Act was signed into law by Bill Clinton. This overarching copyright law criminalizes circumventing access control even if there isn't any actual infringement of the copyright itself. Repairing or modifying your phone is basically illegal. Until 2014[1] it wasn't legal to unlock your phone to switch carriers.
   This is the same DMCA that is being used to take down Youtube videos with no actual violations of copyright. Anyone can claim there is copyrighted material on a video and have that video removed. It's then up to the user that uploaded the video to prove they didn't break any copyright laws to have the video or account reinstated. Guilty until proven innocent.
   Corporations are staying ahead of the curve though... Non-removable batteries are a sort of planned obsolescence. Batteries naturally deteriorate over a couple years and instead of swapping batteries most people are forced to buy a new phone to have it hold a charge for more than a couple hours a day. Apple and Samsung have already been fined for this[2].
   Printers are just as nefarious, embedding chips in the toner and ink cartridges to certify they are officially recognized by the manufacturer. Tractor parts have built-in Digital Rights Management nowadays and off-brand parts can't be used. Luckily, there's a growing number of volunteer hackers reverse-engineering these proprietary parts[3]. And it is technically hackers since circumventing access controls is still illegal...
   Defending the right to repair is a concern that needs addressed before it becomes too serious an issue. Modifying tools and making them better has always been a natural instinct for humans, we are problem solvers. Stymied aptitude of the tools we use, and can't hardly live without, will be a direct consequence of this behavior in the very near future. Turning something off and on again will turn into throw it away and buy another...
