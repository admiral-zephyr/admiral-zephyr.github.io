There is No Spoon
   Over the last couple years a program called Kodi gained a lot of attention. Kodi is open-source home theatre software that runs on a multitude of operating systems including Windows, Linux, OSX, Android and iOS. It was pretty universally supported and that was one of the reasons it came under fire. There was several cases of people selling modified Chromecasts and other brands of TV boxes to not only have Kodi preinstalled but also third party sources. These sources pointed to all manner of stream hosts and a large number of them host pirated shows and movies.
   The Kodi team was under attack for developing a highly customizable video player. They didn't directly block the installation of third party sources but they did threaten legal action against the sell of modified TV boxes with their logo and trademarks on the packaging. These pirate sources would often break and a new source would have to be installed but the person selling the modded boxes would be long gone. This would happen a couple times a month, while it may not be an issue for the experienced computer user, finding these often hidden sources would be near impossible for anyone not savvy with technology. While handled beautifully by Kodi, this still hurt their image. In March of last year, Google blocked 'Kodi' from auto-completing as it was associated with piracy but left the actual pirate sources unchanged...
   Kodi has hung in there though. The latest version, 19, has recently been given a development codename. Users gave suggestions and it was compiled into a top ten list and the votes were cast. Kodi 19 'Matrix' is now official and will be the successor to Kodi 18.2 'Leia'. Not much is known about Kodi 19's features or changes yet and the release date is probably far off. While new features are nice and I like to see continued development, even older versions run smoothly and reliably. Customizing Kodi can be done on many skill levels. You can change skins and other basic options or you can edit the config files yourself manually for a more personal feel. This isn't required but if you're setting up a home theater room with all the bells and whistles then going all out customizing Kodi on a dedicated machine can really enhance your personal theater experience.
   Other ways to improve your setup can be pretty simple too. A remote can be a nice touch and there are plenty of options as well. Kore is the official Kodi remote app but there are several others on both app stores. This is probably the easiest option to set up. Instead of using your smart phone you can also purchase a cheap physical USB remote just as easily for around $10 to $20 on Amazon. The one I was playing around with had a keyboard on the back and a gyro-mouse function. It worked out of the box but some technical knowledge is needed to reprogram the buttons to your liking.
   Tired of changing DVDs? Maybe you could rip them to your hard drive with Handbrake and organize the files with tinyMediaManager. It can take quite a while per disc but having your movies or shows in a digital format on Kodi can look pretty sleek. Have a wireless controller or two? You can also play retro games through Kodi. There's a lot of ways you can take advantage of Kodi, it's a great piece of software and I highly recommend at least trying it out.
