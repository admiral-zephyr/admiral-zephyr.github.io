Rubber Ducky Rebugging
   It's been a pretty quiet week and that gives me little to write about... I've been playing some Super Smash Brothers Ultimate, Fallout 76 and Dwarf Fortress. And there's some Overwatch news. All of which has been pretty mediocre and nothing noteworthy yet. Instead of talking about games this week I figured I could use the downtime to do some updates to the backend to my website and make some hidden improvements. So this article will probably read more like patch notes than a game review or news.
   I've been experimenting with some of Google's web developer tools, mostly Analytics and Search-Console, but there's multitude of widely available services exist to improve websites and organize data. Figuring out what they do and how or how much to implement is the toughest part. I don't have any merchandise to sell, no user accounts and a very small, if any, audience. This should help narrow down what I can add or change. This lead me to research robots.txt and sitemaps, as well as a few other things I never really knew much about but seen in the wild a couple times.
   Web Robots are in use everywhere from Google sniffing for websites to scammers making databases of emails. These little programs, more commonly known as crawlers or spiders, aren't malicious in nature but merely tools that index information gathered from the internet. In the early days of the internet these robots went exploring and swamped servers with repeated requests and discovered virtual folders that shouldn't be public. Many servers adopted to have a file named robots.txt that publicly lists allowed and disallowed directories. Robots are not obligated to follow this list, you cannot hide information by disallowing anything here, it's more of a recommendation. You can add robots.txt to the end of nearly any URL, like www.google.com/robots.txt, and see one for yourself.
   Besides robots.txt, there's also sitemaps, which lists every URL of your website in TXT or XML format. Originally sitemaps were for users, like a directory, but Google now uses them to improve search results. Both of these aren't really seen by most people and can be readily generated automatically by any number of websites on the internet. On the other hand, RSS is kind of like a backwards newsletter. Instead of mailing from a list of people, you provide a XML file that contains URLs to index news or updates for users. Robots.txt, sitemap.xml and rss.xml are three easy ways I improved Nothing Doing without much effort and my hobby site continues to grow as well as my understanding of what it takes to manage one.
